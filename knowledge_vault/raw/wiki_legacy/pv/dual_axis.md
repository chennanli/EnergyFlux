# Dual-axis tracking

Two-axis trackers follow the sun in both azimuth and elevation, keeping the module surface normal to the sun's rays all day, all year.

## Key numbers

* Land density: **~180 kWp DC per acre** at GCR 0.25. The lower density is unavoidable — dual-axis trackers need wider row-to-row spacing to avoid mutual shading as panels tilt in multiple directions.
* Specific yield: **+5% over single-axis bifacial** in best cases. In the central US (~33°N), dual-axis sometimes underperforms single-axis bifacial because the narrower aperture + tracker tilt reduces back-side light collection.
* CAPEX uplift: **+20–30% over single-axis**, driven by the second-axis drive mechanism and O&M complexity.
* Reliability: noticeably lower than single-axis (~98% vs 99%+ uptime). More moving parts, more gearbox failures.

## When it wins (rarely, for US ground-mount utility)

* Very-high-value sites (island grids, military bases, research facilities) where every MWh matters more than CAPEX.
* High-latitude sites (>50°N) where the sun arc makes azimuth tracking materially useful.
* Concentrated PV (CPV) or CPV-like dispatch (not relevant to flat-plane silicon).

## Why it almost never wins for utility-scale in the continental US

The LCOE math: +5% yield × $1.10/W is a smaller dollar per MWh than the +$0.20/W CAPEX uplift dual-axis imposes. It only pencils in niche cases.
